#### **Critical Nuances:**

1. **The Tool-vs-Model Split (NameTag3 & UDPipe2)**: Your findings are spot on, but it is vital for your developers to understand that the **software engine** for both NameTag3 and UDPipe 2 is published under the permissive **MPL 2.0** . However, the pre-trained linguistic models (such as the Czech CNEC models) required to run them are distributed under the restrictive CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 license .  
2. **AISCR Teater Data vs. Software**: While the Teater server system itself is under GPL-3.0, the actual **Thesaurus term database and data files** exported from it are licensed under **CC BY-NC 4.0**. Even if the software pipeline is compliant, utilizing the data within a commercial product will violate this non-commercial boundary.

### **Strategic Recommendation:**

* **The Clean Pipelines**: atrium-page-classification is 100% clean and commercially viable. It uses Apache-2.0 / MIT tools alongside a Public Domain dataset.  
* **The Restricted Pipelines**: Both atrium-nlp-enrich and atrium-translator are heavily entangled with **CC BY-NC-SA** models (such as LINDAT CUBBITT translation and UDPipe tagger models).

### The ATRIUM project is strictly non-commercial by definition, but there is a major distinction between our project's internal use and the usage of our repositories by others

---

#### 1. Internal Project Use: Fully Compliant & Safe

Because our project is entirely funded by public state aid, has no profit motive, and does not seek a "primarily commercial advantage or monetary compensation," our consortium's development, fine-tuning, and execution of these pipelines are **non-commercial by definition**.

Therefore, **within the scope of the ATRIUM grant, our developers are 100% legally permitted** to use:

* **CC BY-NC 4.0 / CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 models and data** (e.g., `FastText` language identification, LINDAT CUBBITT translation models, NameTag3 CNEC taggers, and the AIS CR TEATER vocabulary) .
* **Hosted academic APIs** (such as LINDAT translation and UDPipe parsing APIs) which explicitly restrict usage to non-commercial research and private study.

---

#### 2. External Product Use by Others: The Commercial Barrier

While our project is legally non-commercial, the open-source software, workflows, and tools we build are intended to be shared globally. If we include `CC BY-NC` or `CC BY-NC-SA` components inside our repositories, we create the following compliance boundaries for third parties:

* **Commercial Exclusion:** Any third-party enterprise (e.g., a commercial software company, a private digitization service, or an advertising-funded platform) that downloads and runs our `atrium-translator` or `atrium-nlp-enrich` repositories for monetary gain will **immediately violate** the upstream licenses of the nested NC components .
* **The Share-Alike (SA) Propagation:** Because models like NameTag3's Czech CNEC and LINDAT's CUBBITT translation are governed by `CC BY-NC-SA 4.0`, any downstream pipeline or workflow that integrates, distributes, or creates derivatives of these models **must also be licensed under those exact same non-commercial terms**.

---

### Summary

For **ATRIUM’s internal deliverables and academic demonstrators**, using these tools is entirely safe and compliant. However, if our long-term goal is to allow **unrestricted commercial adoption** of our repositories by external industries, we must actively isolate, containerize, or swap out the `CC BY-NC` and `CC BY-NC-SA` models for permissive, commercially-friendly alternatives (such as Apache 2.0-licensed Qwen or Gemma models).
