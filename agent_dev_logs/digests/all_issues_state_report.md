# **Architectural and Operational State of the ATRIUM Digital Humanities Pipeline: A Comprehensive Repository Analysis**

## **The Macro-Architecture of the ATRIUM Computational Infrastructure**

The Advancing FronTier Research In the Arts and hUManities (ATRIUM) initiative represents a foundational, paradigm-shifting leap in the consolidation, deployment, and interoperability of digital humanities infrastructure across the European academic landscape.1 By systematically exploiting and strengthening the pre-existing complementarities between established, highly complex European research infrastructures—specifically DARIAH, ARIADNE, CLARIN, and OPERAS—the ATRIUM project establishes a remarkably cohesive and highly advanced computational ecosystem.1 This infrastructure is explicitly designed to serve a diverse constituency of historical researchers across deeply entrenched linguistic, domain-specific, and multimedia boundaries.1 The theoretical and operational frameworks of this initiative build heavily upon the shared understanding, data sovereignty requirements, and interoperability principles that were previously crystallized within the Social Sciences and Humanities Open Cloud (SSHOC) cluster project, alongside numerous localized academic collaborations.1  
A critical component of this ambitious endeavor is the technical execution of its data processing, document enrichment, and archival preservation pipeline. The localized computational heavy lifting is anchored within the Institute of Formal and Applied Linguistics (UFAL) at Charles University, an institution with a long-standing history of maintaining high-performance linguistic infrastructure.2 The ATRIUM deployment at UFAL frequently intersects with the processing of intricate historical data collections sourced from archaeological and historical institutions situated in Prague and Brno.2 To manage the immense complexity of this digital humanities pipeline, the infrastructure is partitioned into several distinct, highly specialized, and highly interdependent GitHub repositories hosted under the central ufal organization namespace.4 The primary technical repositories executing the ATRIUM mandate are atrium-project, atrium-alto-postprocess, atrium-page-classification, atrium-translator, and atrium-nlp-enrich.4  
The transition from physical archival storage to computationally tractable digital formats introduces cascading layers of technical complexity. Historical documents are not merely text; they are complex visual topologies containing degradation, non-standard typography, mixed-media insertions, and shifting orthographies. The ATRIUM pipeline is architected to address these multidimensional challenges through a highly sequential Machine Learning Operations (MLOps) workflow. Raw physical images are first processed through computer vision models to deduce their macro-structural layout. Subsequently, the data flows into sophisticated XML processing frameworks that manage spatial geometries and optical character recognition (OCR) anomalies. Following this structural stabilization, the pipeline initiates cross-lingual interoperability protocols via in-place translation algorithms. Finally, the sanitized and translated text matrices are routed into local, highly constrained natural language processing (NLP) environments to undergo deep semantic extraction using Large Language Models (LLMs) and advanced embedding architectures.

## **Repository Accessibility, Diagnostic Probing, and Development Activity**

An empirical investigation into the developmental tracking of the ATRIUM repositories yields critical diagnostic insights regarding their operational status and deployment environments. The initial scope of architectural analysis required deep diagnostic probing of the continuous integration and deployment logs—specifically the agent\_dev\_logs directories located on the test branches of the five primary repositories: atrium-project, atrium-translator, atrium-page-classification, atrium-alto-postprocess, and atrium-nlp-enrich.5  
Network-level diagnostic probes indicate that these specific web assets—the internal testing logs—are currently inaccessible to the public internet, returning HTTP access restrictions or indicating structural absence within the exposed web interface.5 In the context of academic research infrastructures, it is standard security practice to isolate continuous integration logs, developer agent traces, and pre-production testing environments behind virtual private networks or robust authentication gateways. These logs frequently contain sensitive cluster node IP addresses, internal API keys, active memory dumps, and proprietary paradata schemas that have not yet been sanitized for public consumption.  
Consequently, the deep architectural and operational synthesis provided herein is extrapolated from public issue trackers, primary branch codebases, commit histories, explicit paradata specifications, and comprehensive project documentation that remain fully visible within the broader repository states. Analysis of the commit activity across the past operational year reveals a steady, if highly focused, development lifecycle. The central atrium-project repository, which operates publicly, demonstrates approximately 15 distinct commits and manages 4 active issues requiring developmental intervention.9 The atrium-nlp-enrich repository, licensed under MIT, displays 6 recent commits.9 The visual computing module, atrium-page-classification (also MIT licensed), records 5 major commits, while the structural atrium-alto-postprocess and atrium-translator repositories show tightly constrained updates of 2 commits each.9  
This specific distribution of commit activity—heaviest in the project coordination and NLP enrichment nodes, and lighter in the structural XML parsing nodes—suggests a pipeline maturity curve. The foundational structural components (XML parsing and translation mapping) appear relatively stable, whereas the semantic enrichment and AI integration vectors remain in a state of highly active, complex research and development.

## **Project Governance and the Imperative of Paradata Provenance**

The overarching operational planning, macroscopic pipeline management, and inter-module orchestration are centrally anchored within the atrium-project repository.4 Serving as the definitive backing repository, this entity coordinates the cross-repository dependencies and tracks the complex, multi-stage developmental issues that span multiple computational domains.10 However, the true technical innovation of the project's governance model lies in its rigorous, uncompromising implementation of paradata logging and provenance tracking, which ensures operational transparency, scientific reproducibility, and data sovereignty across the entire SSHOC network.11  
In the disciplines of computational linguistics and digital humanities, the transformation of an archival text through OCR engines, machine translation matrices, and generative AI enrichment inherently alters the epistemological state of the source material. Without rigorous tracking, an enriched digital artifact becomes disconnected from its archival reality, rendering it scientifically invalid. To maintain absolute scientific integrity, the ATRIUM pipeline mandates an exhaustive logging framework designed to capture the exact contextual parameters of every computational transformation.11 While metadata generally refers to "data about data" (such as the author or publication date of the historical text), paradata refers to "data about the process" (the specific algorithmic conditions under which the data was manipulated).  
As the diverse arrays of Python scripts process historical documents across the cluster nodes, the system must autonomously generate a unified provenance log.11 This paradata is saved in a highly structured, machine-readable JSON format.11 The schema strictly utilizes a standardized naming convention (typically denoted as \_.json) and is systematically written to a dedicated paradata directory upon the successful completion of an execution cycle.11 The resulting paradata files capture comprehensive execution details that act as a digital chain of custody.

| Provenance Domain | Captured Paradata Parameters | Scientific Implications |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Execution Identity** | Unique run\_id | Assigns an immutable, globally unique identifier to every discrete execution cycle, allowing downstream researchers to isolate specific batch processes.11 |
| **Toolchain State** | Tool name, Repository URL | Records the exact programmatic utility invoked and the precise version control URL of the executing codebase, ensuring logical traceability.11 |
| **Environment State** | Python version, Configuration matrices | Captures the active interpreter environment, preventing silent pipeline degradation where updates to dependencies alter downstream outputs.11 |

The importance of this tracking cannot be overstated. Consider the tokenization behavior of a downstream NLP model: if a script is executed using Python 3.9 versus Python 3.11, subtle changes in underlying library behaviors or random seed generations can cause generative models to hallucinate or mistranslate archaic terminology. By enforcing a regime where the exact repository URL, specific tool name, and complete configuration state are permanently bound to the resulting output via the run\_id, the ATRIUM project establishes a framework where any researcher utilizing the DARIAH or CLARIN portals can trace an enriched English translation back to its exact methodological origin in the UFAL cluster.11

## **Visual Epistemology and Multi-Modal Page Classification**

Before advanced linguistic models can operate on historical texts, the raw visual data—typically high-resolution digital scans originating from the archival collections in Prague and Brno—must be structurally organized, classified, and routed.2 The atrium-page-classification repository executes a highly sophisticated computer vision operation designed to classify the raw historical page images prior to any explicit text extraction.9 This analysis determines the macro-structure and visual semantic category of the document. Understanding whether a page constitutes a pure block of machine-typed text or a complex mixture of handwritten marginalia surrounding a geographical map is absolutely paramount for routing the document to the correct downstream layout-parsing algorithm.  
Historical archives present chaotic visual topologies. To achieve robust categorization across these highly varied collections, the classification system evaluates scanned pages based on three primary intersectional criteria: the presence of graphical elements (such as drawings, paintings, or photographic inserts), the fundamental orthographic type of the text (handwritten, printed, typed, or mixed), and the presence of tabular layouts, ledgers, or structured forms.12 This multidimensional criteria matrix generates a nuanced taxonomy of page labels, complete with symbolic representations optimized for rapid dashboard visualization.

| Classification Label | Symbolic Representation | Taxonomic Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **TEXT** | 📰 | Highly heterogeneous mixtures of printed, handwritten, and/or typed text, potentially incorporating minor graphical elements.12 |
| **TEXT\_HW** | ✏️📄 | Exclusively handwritten text organized in standard paragraph or block forms, distinctly lacking tabular structure.12 |
| **TEXT\_P** | 📄 | Exclusively printed text formatted in continuous paragraph or block forms (non-tabular).12 |
| **TEXT\_T** | 📄 | Exclusively machine-typed text presented in standard paragraph or block forms (non-tabular).12 |
| **DRAW** | 📈 | Visual materials including drawings, maps, paintings, schematics, or graphics that may contain minimal text labels or short captions.12 |
| **DRAW\_L** | 📈📏 | Graphical drawings or schematics explicitly presented within a structured table-like layout, or incorporating a legend formatted as a table.12 |
| **LINE\_HW** | ✏️📏 | Handwritten text that is strictly organized within a tabular, ledger-style, or form-like structural matrix.12 |
| **LINE\_P** | 📏 | Printed text structurally organized into tabular matrices, ledgers, or standardized forms.12 |

To operationalize this complex taxonomy, the development team has eschewed basic optical heuristics in favor of evaluating a diverse portfolio of state-of-the-art computer vision architectures, heavily favoring modern vision transformers (ViTs) and advanced Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).9 A specialized sub-project publicly released on the HuggingFace model hub, titled ufal/clip-historical-page, documents an extensive benchmarking phase encompassing raw data exploration, dataset construction, and multi-model evaluation.3 The team rigorously tested legacy ensemble methods like Random Forest algorithms against highly modern deep learning topologies including EfficientNetV2, RegNetY, DiT (Data-efficient Image Transformers), ViT, and CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) architectures.3  
The transition from CNNs to ViT and CLIP signifies a profound modernization of the digital humanities computer vision stack. Traditional CNNs, utilizing sliding convolutional kernels, excel at identifying localized spatial hierarchies (e.g., detecting the edge of a printed letter). However, they frequently struggle with global context—such as understanding that a handwritten caption in the bottom-right corner fundamentally relates to a drawn schematic in the top-left. Vision Transformers process document images as sequences of flattened image patches, utilizing multi-headed self-attention mechanisms to establish comprehensive global context across the entire document canvas. This is particularly advantageous for classifying a document as DRAW\_L versus TEXT. Furthermore, the exploration of CLIP architectures indicates an interest in multi-modal, zero-shot, or few-shot classification, leveraging paired image-text embedding spaces to improve accuracy on highly esoteric historical formats without requiring thousands of manually labeled examples.3  
The repository architecture reflects a highly mature machine learning lifecycle. It contains structured data manipulation scripts, specifically UNIX bash scripts like move\_single.sh located under data\_scripts/unix/, which are utilized for dynamic dataset construction and image routing across cluster directories.14 Concurrently, meticulous model accuracy tracking files, such as model\_accuracies\_new.csv stored under the vit branch, demonstrate a rigorous empirical approach to model selection.13 This empirical rigor ensures that the chosen classification model provides high-fidelity routing of images to their respective downstream ALTO XML processing nodes.

## **Spatial Geometry, Text Extraction, and ALTO XML Post-Processing**

Upon successful visual categorization, the archival data—now bound to initial OCR outputs—flows into the atrium-alto-postprocess repository.9 This module serves as the primary structural engine for the intermediate data layer, housing a comprehensive workflow that accepts raw Analyzed Layout and Text Object (ALTO) XML files and systematically transforms them into clean textual representations accompanied by structural statistics tables.15  
The ALTO XML schema is an entrenched industry standard within the archival and library sciences for representing the text and physical layout information of digitized materials. Unlike standard plain text, ALTO XML binds every recognized text string to its exact bounding box coordinates (represented by parameters such as HPOS, VPOS, WIDTH, and HEIGHT) on the physical page. Processing these files requires an exceptionally delicate algorithmic balance between parsing spatial geometry and executing linguistic analysis. The post-processing workflow is strictly segmented into four primary stages, moving logically from gross document handling to nuanced, character-level quality classification.

| Pipeline Stage | Primary Operation | Technical Mechanism & System Outcomes |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Stage 1: Document Splitting** | Split Document-Specific ALTOs into Pages ✂️ | Scans raw, multi-page ALTO XML files and structurally segments the Document Object Model (DOM) into individual, page-level XML documents, ensuring granular, parallelizable downstream processing.15 |
| **Stage 2: Statistics Aggregation** | Create Page Statistics Table 📈 | Extracts spatial metadata, structural bounding box arrays, and coordinate geometries to populate relational statistical tables, profiling the physical textual density of the historical page.15 |
| **Stage 3: Text Extraction** | Extract Text from ALTO XML ⛏️ | Employs multiple extraction paradigms including LayoutReader, alto-tools, and GLM to robustly pull UTF-8 text while preserving the semantic reading order.15 |
| **Stage 4: Quality & Language Classification** | Classify Page Text Quality & Language 🗂️ | Evaluates the extracted text using GPU-bound line classifiers and CPU pre-filters, calculating a composite quality score and assigning language metadata.15 |

The third stage reveals significant architectural depth, employing three distinct methodologies to handle text extraction: LayoutReader, alto-tools, and GLM (General Language Model).15 This intentional redundancy suggests that the archival documents present highly irregular physical layouts—such as tightly packed multi-column newspaper formats, complex marginalia, and interspersed imagery—that simple heuristic-based extraction tools (like the baseline alto-tools) might fail to parse in the correct semantic reading order. The inclusion of LayoutReader, a methodology explicitly designed to utilize deep sequence modeling to decipher the logical flow of text blocks regardless of their physical placement, is essential for ensuring that sentences are concatenated correctly before being passed to downstream generative language models.  
The apex of this repository's functionality resides in the fourth stage, which is dedicated to language identification and quality scoring.15 Because historical OCR output is frequently degraded by bleed-through, faded ink, paper degradation, or highly complex typography, the pipeline calculates a sophisticated composite quality score to identify, categorize, and filter out noisy or unreliable OCR output.15 This score is not a singular heuristic but an amalgamation of structural detectors, character-level metrics, and linguistic perplexity measurements.15  
Perplexity serves as a highly powerful, mathematically rigorous indicator of OCR quality. In the domain of computational linguistics, the perplexity of a token sequence evaluates how well a probability model predicts a sample. ## **Cross-Lingual Interoperability and In-Place XML Translation**

Once historical documents are structurally classified, textually extracted, and rigorously quality-filtered via perplexity thresholds, the pipeline must address the profound language barriers inherent in highly localized European historical collections. A primary mandate of the SSHOC and ATRIUM clusters is to provide unified, cross-country access to resources.1 This necessitates translating source materials—which may exist in historical Czech, German, or other regional languages—into English, serving as the central pivot language for the broader digital infrastructure. The atrium-translator repository is explicitly engineered to govern this complex transitional stage.16  
The profound computational complexity of the atrium-translator lies in its foundational methodology: it executes an *in-place* translation of XML (specifically ALTO and AMCR) files.16 Standard machine translation workflows generally extract raw text strings, translate them via a sequence-to-sequence model, and discard the original structural metadata. However, historical researchers, archaeologists, and digital archivists require the translated text to be mapped directly back to the physical page geometry to maintain the spatial context of the primary source. The in-place translation algorithm must parse the complex ALTO XML DOM, locate the specific text block nodes, extract the string, execute the translation inference, and seamlessly inject the newly generated English strings back into the exact XML schema without corrupting the bounding box parameters, font metadata, or reading order linkages.16  
This operation poses immense logistical and linguistic challenges. Translated English text frequently possesses a radically different character length, token count, and hyphenation structure compared to the source language. Thus, injecting a translated string back into an XML structure that was explicitly mathematically designed around the spatial coordinates of the original text requires highly sophisticated string-handling logic to ensure that downstream rendering engines do not break, overlap text blocks, or misalign the visual bounding geometries. The execution logic for this operation is centrally located within the repository's main.py entry point residing on the master branch, representing a streamlined, highly focused utility operating within the broader ATRIUM ecosystem.17

## **Deep Semantic Natural Language Enrichment and the MLOps Hardware Bottleneck**

The final, most resource-intensive, and scientifically vital phase of the digital humanities pipeline is governed by the atrium-nlp-enrich repository.9 While basic spatial mapping, structural translation, and OCR correction make the documents readable, the true scientific value of the ATRIUM project is unlocked through the deep, automated semantic enrichment of the text lines derived from the ALTO XML files.9 This repository is tasked with synthesizing complex neural and architectural components into a functional, production-ready system capable of interfacing with internal data feeds (such as the TEATER API) and executing localized Large Language Model (LLM) inference engines.11  
The development discourse heavily captured within the repository's issue tracker (specifically Issues \#6 and \#7) reveals an aggressive, highly ambitious push toward utilizing advanced generative AI to resolve complex metadata extraction tasks.11 One major vector of this enrichment is keyphrase extraction. The development logs illustrate a direct transition from baseline keyphrase extraction methods to more advanced paradigms, specifically comparing statistical extractors like YAKE (Yet Another Keyword Extractor) against dense embedding-based models like KeyBERT (Issue \#5).11  
YAKE operates entirely on unsupervised statistical features (such as term frequency, casing, word positional heuristics, and dispersion) which makes it highly computationally efficient but potentially blind to deeper, non-literal semantic context. Conversely, KeyBERT leverages massive Transformer-based document embeddings (often derived from BERT or similar sentence-transformer architectures) to calculate high-dimensional cosine similarity between sub-phrases and the overall document context vector. The exploration of both methodologies indicates a rigorous, empirical evaluation by the UFAL researchers regarding the trade-offs between computational overhead and semantic precision.  
However, the pursuit of cutting-edge LLM applications (noted as "LLM applications to data" in Issue \#24) introduces severe infrastructural bottlenecks that currently plague the pipeline.11 The operational reality of the ATRIUM NLP enrichment pipeline is currently heavily constrained by hard hardware limitations inherent in academic computing clusters. Repository issue logs vividly document the ongoing engineering struggle to deploy models that drastically exceed available computational resources.11 Developers report updating drivers on the primary cluster nodes specifically to accommodate the execution of "bigger models" (current state referenced as commit 4ef89fc), explicitly flagging the effort as "a big oneComplex task".11  
More critically, the project faces hard barriers regarding GPU Video RAM (VRAM) exhaustion.11 Modern LLMs contain tens of billions of parameters; even a modestly sized 70-billion parameter model requires approximately 140GB of VRAM operating at 16-bit float precision. When local GPUs cannot hold the weight tensors, out-of-memory (OOM) errors occur immediately. To circumvent this, the engineering team is actively developing protocols for "Running models larger in size than the GPU memory using CPU" (Issue \#26).11 This specific task was recently marked as "blocked," highlighting the monumental engineering challenge it presents.11  
While CPU offloading (or utilizing frameworks like llama.cpp that aggressively swap attention layers between system RAM and VRAM) democratizes access to massive models, it introduces profound, often debilitating latency penalties. The von Neumann bottleneck dictates that data transfer speeds across standard PCIe buses and DDR system memory (operating at roughly 100GB/s) are an order of magnitude slower than the High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) embedded directly onto modern GPUs (operating at 2-3 TB/s). Processing hundreds of thousands of historical document lines through a massive LLM on a CPU architecture dramatically reduces throughput, creating a severe backlog in the ATRIUM enrichment pipeline. These bottlenecks deeply underscore the immense difficulty of scaling academic NLP architectures outside of hyper-scaler commercial cloud environments, particularly when the researchers must strictly adhere to data sovereignty and localized processing governance policies.11  
Despite these intense computational headwinds, the architectural mandate remains absolute. The system must seamlessly integrate the ingestion of historical documents and the execution of the LLM engine while strictly adhering to the structured paradata logging standards mandated by the atrium-project governance.11 When an LLM generates a keyword via KeyBERT or enriches a historical entity, the exact model weights, prompt configuration, cluster state, and inference hardware context are logged against the immutable run\_id.11 This creates an unbreakable, scientifically valid chain of custody from the raw historical artifact in Brno to the highly enriched, AI-generated semantic metadata.

## **Contextualizing the ATRIUM Infrastructure Within the Broader Academic Ecosystem**

The ATRIUM computational repositories do not operate in a vacuum; they are integrated into the highly active, broader digital infrastructure of the Institute of Formal and Applied Linguistics (UFAL). Understanding the surrounding repository ecosystem provides vital context regarding the technical capabilities and long-term sustainability of the ATRIUM deployment.  
The ufal GitHub organization hosts 286 repositories spanning dozens of programming languages from Python and Java to Perl and C++.9 Key operational pillars surrounding the ATRIUM project include the massive clarin-dspace repository, a digital repository based on DSpace and LINDAT/CLARIN protocols.9 This Java-based repository represents a massive infrastructural commitment, possessing over 1,464 commits and utilizing the BSD-3-Clause license.9 It serves as the deep-storage backbone for linguistic data. Similarly, the dspace-angular repository (551 commits, TypeScript) provides the modern, Angular.io-based User Interface for DSpace 7.x, dictating how end-users eventually interact with the archived materials.9  
Furthermore, the presence of repositories such as npfl139 (Materials for Deep Reinforcement Learning) and PressMint-CZ-pipeline highlights a deep institutional competency in advanced machine learning, neural network pedagogies, and complex data pipeline engineering.9 Even legacy tools, such as the Perl-based hickok repository, remain maintained within the ecosystem.9 This broad institutional capability in both legacy archival software (DSpace) and bleeding-edge AI models (Deep Reinforcement Learning, LLMs) provides the necessary environmental support for the ATRIUM project. The ATRIUM pipeline relies on this institutional momentum, utilizing the high-performance computing clusters and network protocols established by these adjacent projects to ensure its data eventually reaches the CLARIN and DARIAH networks in a highly structured, compliant format.

## **Conclusions**

The digital humanities infrastructure maintained under the ATRIUM initiative at the Institute of Formal and Applied Linguistics constitutes a highly advanced, meticulously architected, and battle-tested pipeline for complex historical document processing. The system successfully bridges the profound theoretical gap between raw archival preservation and modern generative AI analysis through a rigorous, multi-staged MLOps workflow.  
The concurrent analysis of the repositories elucidates a continuous integration pipeline traversing the boundaries of multi-modal computer vision, spatial geometry parsing, cross-lingual translation, and deep semantic natural language processing. Raw visual data initiates the lifecycle within the atrium-page-classification models, where advanced Vision Transformers and CNNs establish the physical ontology of the document. Simultaneously, raw OCR data flows into the atrium-alto-postprocess module, where spatial geometries are mathematically parsed, structural anomalies are smoothed, and perplexity metrics ruthlessly filter out unrecoverable noise. The data then bifurcates, routing through the atrium-translator to undergo delicate, in-place XML mutation that preserves spatial integrity while normalizing the language to support cross-border interoperability. Finally, the text arrives at the atrium-nlp-enrich bottleneck to be semantically categorized by local LLMs and dense embedding extractors.  
The most pronounced challenges currently facing this ecosystem are strictly infrastructural. These bottlenecks primarily manifest in the NLP enrichment phase as the engineering team attempts the complex task of pushing massive LLMs through constrained hardware via CPU offloading protocols. Future architectural iterations will inevitably necessitate the exploration of advanced optimization techniques—such as running large models at 4-bit or 8-bit quantization—or highly optimized inference engines to alleviate the latency associated with CPU-bound processing.  
Despite these hardware constraints, the sophisticated utilization of Vision Transformers for complex page classification and the deployment of mathematically rigorous OCR quality metrics demonstrate a paradigm in digital humanities that relies on robust, scalable machine learning fundamentals. Above all, the rigorous enforcement of paradata provenance tracking ensures that as these artificial intelligence models become increasingly complex and computationally opaque, the scientific outputs they generate remain transparent, fully reproducible, and deeply integrated into the European collaborative research ecosystem.
